1. What is qPCR?

PCR Basics

Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplifies a specific DNA sequence through repeated cycles of denaturation, annealing, and extension. Each cycle roughly doubles the target, producing an exponential increase in copy number.

Quantitative Real-Time PCR

Quantitative PCR (qPCR) adds a fluorescent reporter to standard PCR so that amplification can be monitored in real time, cycle by cycle. Fluorescence intensity is proportional to the amount of amplified product.

Common chemistries include SYBR Green (intercalating dye, binds any double-stranded DNA) and TaqMan probes (sequence-specific hydrolysis probes with a fluorophore and quencher).

Amplification Curves & the Ct Value

A plot of fluorescence vs. cycle number produces a sigmoidal amplification curve. The Ct (cycle threshold) — also called Cq — is the fractional cycle number at which fluorescence crosses a defined threshold in the exponential phase.

Key insight: Lower Ct = more starting template. A Ct difference of 1 cycle corresponds to roughly a 2-fold difference in initial quantity (assuming 100% efficiency).

2. The ΔΔCt Method (Livak)

The ΔΔCt method, published by Livak & Schmittgen (2001), is the most widely used approach for calculating relative gene expression changes. It compares a target gene's expression between experimental and control samples, normalized to a reference (housekeeping) gene.

Step 1 — Calculate ΔCt

For each sample, subtract the reference gene Ct from the target gene Ct:

ΔCt = Cttarget − Ctreference

This normalizes for differences in cDNA input between samples.

Step 2 — Calculate ΔΔCt

Subtract the mean ΔCt of the control group from each sample's ΔCt:

ΔΔCt = ΔCtsample − mean(ΔCtcontrol)

This expresses each sample's expression relative to the control baseline.

Step 3 — Calculate Fold Change

Convert to a linear fold change value:

Fold Change = 2−ΔΔCt

A fold change of 1.0 means no change from control. Values > 1 indicate upregulation; values < 1 indicate downregulation. For example, fold change = 4 means the gene is expressed 4× higher in the sample than in the control.

3. Reference Genes

Reference genes (also called housekeeping genes or endogenous controls) are genes assumed to be expressed at a constant level across all experimental conditions. They serve as internal normalizers to correct for sample-to-sample variation in RNA extraction, reverse transcription efficiency, and cDNA loading.

Choosing a Reference Gene

  • Verify stable expression across your experimental conditions
  • Use validation tools (geNorm, NormFinder, BestKeeper) when possible
  • Using multiple reference genes (geometric mean) improves reliability
  • Never assume a gene is stable — always validate empirically

4. Quality Control

Replicate Consistency (CV%)

The coefficient of variation (CV%) measures replicate reproducibility. qPCRCalc flags replicates automatically:

CV%StatusAction
≤ 2%GoodAcceptable — proceed
2–5%SuspiciousReview replicates; consider re-running
> 5%UnreliableDiscard or investigate pipetting error

High Ct Values

⚠ Ct > 35: At very high cycle numbers, amplification curves flatten and non-specific products or primer dimers can dominate. Ct values above 35 should be interpreted with caution — they may indicate very low expression, failed reactions, or absent template.

Other QC Checks

  • No-template controls (NTC) should show no amplification or Ct > 38
  • Melt curve analysis (SYBR Green) — single peak confirms specificity
  • Amplification efficiency — ideally 90–110% from a standard curve

5. Error Propagation

SEM Calculation

The standard error of the mean (SEM) quantifies uncertainty in the mean ΔCt across biological replicates:

SEM = SD / √n

where SD is the standard deviation of ΔCt values and n is the number of replicates.

Fold Change Error Bars

Because fold change is computed via exponentiation (2−ΔΔCt), error bars are propagated through the power function:

Upper bound = 2−(ΔΔCt − SEM)
Lower bound = 2−(ΔΔCt + SEM)

Why Are Error Bars Asymmetric?

The exponential transformation converts a symmetric interval on the log scale (ΔΔCt ± SEM) into an asymmetric interval on the linear scale. This is expected and correct — it reflects the multiplicative nature of fold changes. The upper bar is always wider than the lower bar.

6. Assumptions & Limitations

The ΔΔCt method assumes:

  1. 100% amplification efficiency — each cycle exactly doubles the target. The factor "2" in 2−ΔΔCt comes from this assumption.
  2. Equal efficiency for target and reference genes.
  3. Stable reference gene expression across all conditions.
  4. Linear dynamic range — Ct values fall in the exponential amplification phase.

When ΔΔCt Breaks Down

  • Unequal efficiencies: If target and reference differ by > 5%, use the Pfaffl method: Fold = (Etarget)ΔCt_target / (Eref)ΔCt_ref
  • Very low expression: Ct > 35 introduces stochastic noise
  • Unstable reference: Results are only as good as your normalizer
  • Non-biological variation: Batch effects, plate-to-plate variation need careful experimental design
